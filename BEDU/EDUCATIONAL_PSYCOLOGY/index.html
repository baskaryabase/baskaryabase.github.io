EDUCATIONAL PSYCOLOGY

UNIT – I : PSYCHOLOGY & LEARNING PROCESS​

1. Meaning & Definition of Psychology​

Meaning​

Psychology is the scientific study of human behaviour and mental processes. It studies how people think, feel, learn, and behave in different situations.

The word Psychology is derived from two Greek words:

  • Psyche – Soul / Mind
  • Logos – Study

So, Psychology means the study of the mind and behaviour.

Definitions​

  1. William James: “Psychology is the science of mental life, both of its phenomena and their conditions.”

  2. Crow and Crow: “Psychology is the study of human behaviour and human relationship.”

  3. Woodworth: “Psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and activities of living organisms.”

👉 Conclusion: Psychology helps us understand individual differences, learning abilities, emotions, and behaviour patterns.

2. Educational Psychology – Meaning, Scope & Importance​

Meaning of Educational Psychology​

Educational Psychology is a branch of psychology that deals with the behaviour of learners in educational situations. It applies psychological principles to improve teaching–learning processes.

Definition

  • Skinner: “Educational psychology is the science of behaviour of the learner in educational situations.”

Scope of Educational Psychology​

The scope is very wide and includes:

  1. Growth and Development of Learners
  2. Individual Differences
  3. Learning Process and Learning Theories
  4. Motivation and Attention
  5. Intelligence, Creativity, and Aptitude
  6. Personality and Adjustment
  7. Mental Health and Guidance
  8. Measurement and Evaluation

Importance of Educational Psychology​

Educational Psychology helps the teacher to:

  1. Understand the nature of learners
  2. Select appropriate teaching methods
  3. Deal with slow learners and gifted students
  4. Motivate students effectively
  5. Improve classroom management
  6. Evaluate students properly
  7. Make teaching student-centred

👉 Hence, Educational Psychology is essential for effective teaching and learning.

3. Relationship between Psychology and Education​

Psychology and Education are closely related and interdependent.

Relationship Explained​

PsychologyEducation
Studies human behaviourApplies behaviour principles
Provides theories of learningUses theories in teaching
Explains how learning occursOrganises learning experiences

Key Points​

  1. Psychology provides scientific foundation for education
  2. Education applies psychological principles in practice
  3. Psychology explains how students learn, education decides what to teach
  4. Effective teaching is not possible without psychology

👉 Conclusion: Psychology is the base, education is the application.

4. Role of Educational Psychology in Teaching–Learning Process​

Educational Psychology plays a vital role in making teaching effective.

Role in Teaching–Learning​

  1. Understanding Learners Helps teachers understand students’ abilities, interests, and needs.

  2. Selection of Teaching Methods Helps choose suitable methods like lecture, activity-based learning, etc.

  3. Motivation of Learners Helps teachers motivate students using rewards, reinforcement, and encouragement.

  4. Handling Individual Differences Helps in dealing with slow learners, average learners, and gifted learners.

  5. Classroom Management Helps maintain discipline and create a positive learning environment.

  6. Evaluation and Assessment Helps in preparing tests and measuring learning outcomes.

👉 Thus, Educational Psychology improves quality of education.

5. Methods of Psychology​

Psychology uses scientific methods to study behaviour.

(a) Introspection Method​

Meaning Introspection means looking within oneself to study one’s own thoughts, feelings, and emotions.

Features

  • Self-observation
  • Subjective method

Merits

  • Simple and direct
  • Useful in studying feelings and emotions

Demerits

  • Not reliable
  • Cannot be used with children

(b) Observation Method​

Meaning Observation method studies behaviour by watching and recording actions of individuals in natural situations.

Types

  • Natural observation
  • Controlled observation

Merits

  • Objective method
  • Useful for children

Demerits

  • Time-consuming
  • Observer bias possible

(c) Experimental Method​

Meaning In this method, behaviour is studied under controlled conditions by changing variables.

Steps

  1. Formulation of hypothesis
  2. Experimentation
  3. Observation
  4. Conclusion

Merits

  • Scientific and accurate
  • Establishes cause-effect relationship

Demerits

  • Artificial conditions
  • Not suitable for all problems

(d) Survey Method​

Meaning Survey method collects information from a large group using questionnaires or interviews.

Tools Used

  • Questionnaire
  • Interview
  • Rating scales

Merits

  • Covers large population
  • Saves time

Demerits

  • Responses may not be accurate
  • Limited depth

UNIT – II : GROWTH & DEVELOPMENT

1. Meaning of Growth and Development​

Growth – Meaning​

Growth refers to the physical and quantitative changes in the body of an individual. It includes increase in height, weight, size, and strength.

Characteristics of Growth

  • Physical in nature
  • Measurable
  • Limited up to maturity
  • External changes

Definition

  • Crow and Crow: “Growth refers to structural and physiological changes.”

Development – Meaning​

Development refers to overall changes in an individual including physical, mental, emotional, social, and moral aspects.

Characteristics of Development

  • Both qualitative and quantitative
  • Continuous process
  • Lifelong
  • Internal as well as external changes

Definition

  • Hurlock: “Development is a progressive series of changes that occur as a result of maturation and experience.”

2. Difference between Growth and Development​

GrowthDevelopment
Physical changes onlyOverall personality changes
QuantitativeQualitative and quantitative
MeasurableNot always measurable
Stops after maturityContinues throughout life
Limited scopeWide scope

👉 Exam Tip: This table is very important for 5 & 10 marks.

3. Principles of Growth & Development​

Human growth and development follow certain universal principles.

Important Principles​

  1. Development is Continuous Development starts from birth and continues till death.

  2. Development Proceeds from General to Specific Example: A child moves arms first, then fingers.

  3. Development Follows a Definite Pattern

    • Head to toe (Cephalocaudal)
    • Centre to periphery (Proximodistal)

  4. Rate of Development Differs among Individuals Each child grows at his/her own pace.

  5. Development is the Result of Maturation and Learning Both heredity and environment influence development.

  6. Development is Predictable Stages of development occur in a fixed sequence.

👉 These principles help teachers understand learners better.

4. Stages of Development​

Human development is divided into different stages for easy understanding.

(a) Infancy (Birth to 2 years)​

Characteristics

  • Rapid physical growth
  • Development of sense organs
  • Dependency on parents
  • Emotional attachment

Educational Significance

  • Proper nutrition is essential
  • Emotional security is important

(b) Childhood (3 to 12 years)​

Characteristics

  • Steady physical growth
  • Development of language
  • Curiosity and imagination
  • Formation of habits

Educational Significance

  • Best period for learning
  • Activity-based teaching is effective

(c) Adolescence (13 to 18 years)​

Characteristics

  • Rapid physical and emotional changes
  • Development of abstract thinking
  • Emotional instability
  • Desire for independence

Educational Significance

  • Guidance and counselling are essential
  • Moral and emotional support needed

(d) Adulthood (19 years onwards)​

Characteristics

  • Physical growth stabilizes
  • Emotional maturity
  • Social responsibility
  • Career and family life

Educational Significance

  • Vocational education important
  • Lifelong learning continues

5. Factors Influencing Development​

Human development is influenced by many factors. The two major factors are:

(a) Heredity​

Meaning Heredity refers to the genetic traits passed from parents to children.

Influence of Heredity

  • Physical characteristics (height, weight)
  • Intelligence
  • Talents and abilities
  • Temperament

Role in Development

  • Sets the potential of an individual
  • Cannot be changed

(b) Environment​

Meaning Environment includes all external factors affecting an individual.

Types of Environment

  • Home environment
  • School environment
  • Social environment
  • Cultural environment

Influence of Environment

  • Shapes behaviour
  • Develops personality
  • Improves or limits potential

Relationship between Heredity and Environment​

  • Heredity provides capacity
  • Environment provides opportunity
  • Development is the result of interaction between both

👉 “Heredity loads the gun, environment pulls the trigger.”

🔥 UNIT III – LEARNING (MOST IMPORTANT)

1. Meaning & Definition of Learning​

Meaning of Learning​

Learning is a process through which behaviour changes as a result of experience, practice, or training. It brings relatively permanent change in behaviour.

Learning is not just acquiring knowledge; it includes skills, habits, attitudes, and values.

Definitions of Learning​

  1. E.L. Thorndike “Learning is the formation of bonds or connections between stimulus and response.”

  2. Skinner “Learning is a process of progressive behaviour adaptation.”

  3. Gates “Learning is the modification of behaviour through experience.”

👉 Exam line: Learning results in permanent behavioural change.

2. Characteristics of Learning​

  1. Learning is a continuous process
  2. Learning results in change in behaviour
  3. Learning is goal-oriented
  4. Learning is based on experience and practice
  5. Learning is universal (applies to all individuals)
  6. Learning is individualistic (differs from person to person)
  7. Learning is both conscious and unconscious

3. Types of Learning​

Learning is classified into three domains.

(a) Cognitive Learning​

Meaning Cognitive learning relates to knowledge, thinking, reasoning, memory, and problem-solving.

Examples

  • Learning concepts
  • Understanding principles
  • Solving mathematics problems

Educational Significance

  • Develops intelligence
  • Improves reasoning ability

(b) Affective Learning​

Meaning Affective learning deals with emotions, feelings, attitudes, interests, and values.

Examples

  • Developing positive attitude
  • Moral values
  • Emotional responses

Educational Significance

  • Character formation
  • Emotional stability

(c) Psychomotor Learning​

Meaning Psychomotor learning involves physical skills and muscular coordination.

Examples

  • Writing
  • Drawing
  • Sports skills

Educational Significance

  • Skill development
  • Physical coordination

4. Theories of Learning​

(a) Trial and Error Theory – Thorndike​

Proposed by: E.L. Thorndike

Meaning​

Learning occurs through repeated attempts. Wrong responses are eliminated and the correct response is strengthened.

Experiment (Puzzle Box)​

  • Cat placed in puzzle box
  • Random movements at first
  • Gradually learns correct response

Educational Implications​

  1. Practice improves learning
  2. Motivation is important
  3. Learning by doing

(b) Classical Conditioning – Pavlov​

Proposed by: Ivan Pavlov

Meaning​

Learning occurs by association between stimulus and response.

Experiment​

  • Food → Salivation
  • Bell + Food → Salivation
  • Bell alone → Salivation

Educational Implications​

  1. Habit formation
  2. Emotional conditioning
  3. Classroom atmosphere influences learning

(c) Operant Conditioning – Skinner​

Proposed by: B.F. Skinner

Meaning​

Learning occurs through reinforcement of responses.

Types of Reinforcement​

  • Positive reinforcement (reward)
  • Negative reinforcement (removal of unpleasant stimulus)

Educational Implications​

  1. Rewards motivate learning
  2. Immediate feedback is effective
  3. Punishment should be avoided

(d) Insight Learning – Köhler​

Proposed by: Wolfgang Köhler

Meaning​

Learning occurs suddenly through insight, not trial and error.

Experiment (Chimpanzee & Banana)​

  • Sudden solution
  • Understanding relationships

Educational Implications​

  1. Encourages problem-solving
  2. Understanding is important
  3. Rote learning should be avoided

5. Laws of Learning – Thorndike​

Primary Laws​

  1. Law of Readiness Learning occurs when learner is mentally and physically ready.

  2. Law of Exercise Practice strengthens learning; disuse weakens learning.

  3. Law of Effect Responses followed by satisfaction are strengthened.

Secondary Laws​

  1. Law of Multiple Response
  2. Law of Set or Attitude
  3. Law of Assimilation
  4. Law of Associative Shifting

👉 Law of Effect = MOST IMPORTANT for exams

6. Factors Affecting Learning​

Learner-related Factors​

  1. Intelligence
  2. Motivation
  3. Interest
  4. Attention
  5. Physical and mental health

Teacher-related Factors​

  1. Teaching methods
  2. Personality of teacher
  3. Teacher-student relationship

Environmental Factors​

  1. Classroom environment
  2. Home environment
  3. Teaching aids

7. Transfer of Learning​

Meaning​

Transfer of learning refers to the application of knowledge or skills learned in one situation to another situation.

Types of Transfer of Learning​

  1. Positive Transfer Previous learning helps new learning Example: Learning Tamil helps learning Telugu

  2. Negative Transfer Previous learning interferes with new learning Example: Wrong pronunciation habits

  3. Zero Transfer No effect of previous learning

  4. Near Transfer Similar situations

  5. Far Transfer Different situations

⭐ Scoring Unit – Prepare well​

PART – A : INTELLIGENCE​

1. Intelligence – Meaning & Definition​

Meaning​

Intelligence is the ability to learn, understand, reason, solve problems, and adapt to new situations.

Definitions​

  1. Alfred Binet “Intelligence is the ability to judge well, reason well, and act wisely.”

  2. Wechsler “Intelligence is the global capacity of an individual to act purposefully, think rationally, and deal effectively with the environment.”

2. Types of Intelligence​

  1. Abstract Intelligence – dealing with ideas and symbols
  2. Concrete Intelligence – dealing with objects and things
  3. Social Intelligence – understanding and managing people
  4. Emotional Intelligence – understanding emotions

3. Theories of Intelligence​

(a) Spearman’s Two-Factor Theory​

Proposed by: Charles Spearman

Factors

  • G factor – General intelligence (common to all activities)
  • S factor – Specific intelligence (task-specific)

👉 Intelligence = G + S

(b) Thurstone’s Group Factor Theory​

Proposed by: L.L. Thurstone

He identified Primary Mental Abilities, such as:

  1. Verbal comprehension
  2. Numerical ability
  3. Spatial ability
  4. Memory
  5. Reasoning
  6. Word fluency

(c) Gardner’s Multiple Intelligence Theory​

Proposed by: Howard Gardner

He proposed 8 types of intelligence:

  1. Linguistic
  2. Logical–Mathematical
  3. Spatial
  4. Musical
  5. Bodily–Kinesthetic
  6. Interpersonal
  7. Intrapersonal
  8. Naturalistic

👉 Educational value: Every child is intelligent in different ways.

4. Measurement of Intelligence – IQ​

Formula (Binet)​

Classification​

  • Above 120 – Superior
  • 90–109 – Average
  • Below 70 – Intellectually challenged

PART – B : CREATIVITY

5. Creativity – Meaning & Characteristics​

Meaning​

Creativity is the ability to produce something new, original, and useful.

Characteristics of Creative Person​

  1. Original thinking
  2. Fluency of ideas
  3. Flexibility
  4. Imagination
  5. Curiosity
  6. Problem-solving ability

6. Factors Influencing Creativity​

  1. Intelligence
  2. Motivation
  3. Environment
  4. Freedom of expression
  5. Teacher encouragement
  6. Emotional security

7. Role of Teacher in Developing Creativity​

  1. Encourage originality
  2. Avoid over-criticism
  3. Use activity-based learning
  4. Provide free and flexible environment
  5. Encourage questioning
  6. Appreciate creative efforts

👉 Teacher is the key facilitator of creativity

PART – C : PERSONALITY

8. Personality – Meaning & Definition​

Meaning​

Personality is the totality of an individual’s behaviour, including physical, mental, emotional, and social traits.

Definitions​

  1. Allport: “Personality is the dynamic organization within the individual of those psychophysical systems that determine his unique adjustment to the environment.”

  2. Morton Prince: “Personality is the sum total of biological, innate, and acquired dispositions.”

9. Types of Personality​

  1. Introvert – quiet, inward-looking
  2. Extrovert – social, outgoing
  3. Ambivert – balanced type

10. Theories of Personality​

(a) Freud’s Psychoanalytic Theory​

Proposed by: Sigmund Freud

Structure of Personality

  1. Id – instinctive, pleasure principle
  2. Ego – reality principle
  3. Superego – moral conscience

👉 Behaviour is influenced by unconscious mind

(b) Jung’s Theory of Personality​

Proposed by: Carl Jung

He classified personality into:

  • Introverts
  • Extroverts

He also emphasized collective unconscious.

11. Assessment of Personality​

Methods​

  1. Observation method

  2. Interview method

  3. Questionnaire

  4. Rating scales

  5. Projective techniques

    • Rorschach Inkblot Test
    • Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)

UNIT–V : Motivation, Memory & Individual Differences

(B.Ed 1st Year – Educational Psychology | Tamil Nadu Syllabus)

PART – A : MOTIVATION

1. Motivation – Meaning & Definition​

Meaning​

Motivation is the inner drive or force that stimulates a person to act or learn. It directs behaviour towards achieving goals.

Definitions​

  1. Good “Motivation is the process of arousing, sustaining and directing behaviour.”

  2. Skinner Motivation is reinforcement that strengthens behaviour.

2. Types of Motivation​

(a) Intrinsic Motivation​

  • Comes from within the learner
  • Learning for interest, curiosity, satisfaction
  • Example: Reading a book for pleasure

👉 Strong and long-lasting motivation

(b) Extrinsic Motivation​

  • Comes from external rewards
  • Rewards, marks, prizes, punishment
  • Example: Studying for high marks

👉 Short-term motivation

3. Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs​

Proposed by: Abraham Maslow

Maslow arranged human needs in a pyramid form.

Five Levels​

  1. Physiological Needs – food, water, sleep
  2. Safety Needs – protection, security
  3. Love & Belongingness – friendship, affection
  4. Esteem Needs – respect, recognition
  5. Self-Actualization – achieving full potential

👉 Lower needs must be satisfied before higher needs.

4. Role of Motivation in Learning​

  1. Creates interest
  2. Improves concentration
  3. Increases achievement
  4. Encourages active participation
  5. Reduces boredom
  6. Builds confidence

👉 “Motivation is the key to learning.”

PART – B : MEMORY

5. Memory – Meaning & Types​

Meaning​

Memory is the ability to store, retain, and recall information when needed.

Types of Memory​

  1. Sensory Memory – very short duration
  2. Short-Term Memory (STM) – temporary storage
  3. Long-Term Memory (LTM) – permanent storage

6. Forgetting – Causes​

  1. Lack of practice
  2. Interference
  3. Emotional disturbance
  4. Poor attention
  5. Brain injury
  6. Passage of time

👉 Forgetting is natural if learning is not reinforced.

7. Improving Memory​

  1. Repetition and practice
  2. Understanding instead of rote learning
  3. Use of mnemonics
  4. Proper sleep
  5. Interest and motivation
  6. Spaced learning method

PART – C : INDIVIDUAL DIFFERENCES

8. Meaning of Individual Differences​

Individual differences refer to variations among individuals in physical, mental, emotional, and social characteristics.

No two individuals are exactly alike.

9. Areas of Individual Differences​

  1. Intelligence
  2. Aptitude
  3. Interest
  4. Attitude
  5. Personality
  6. Physical abilities

(a) Intelligence​

Ability to think, reason, and solve problems.

(b) Aptitude​

Special ability in a particular area (music, maths, etc.)

(c) Interest​

Liking or preference towards certain activities.

(d) Attitude​

Positive or negative feelings towards something.

10. Role of Teacher in Handling Individual Differences​

  1. Use varied teaching methods
  2. Provide individual attention
  3. Identify slow and gifted learners
  4. Encourage all students
  5. Use remedial teaching
  6. Maintain inclusive classroom
  7. Provide guidance and counselling

👉 Teacher must treat every learner as unique.
